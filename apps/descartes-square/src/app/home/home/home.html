What Is Descartes' Square?

Descartes' Square is a simple yet powerful decision-making technique. It helps you examine any complex situation from four different angles, helping you avoid emotional bias and uncover hidden consequences.

Named after the French philosopher René Descartes, the method encourages you to stop worrying and start analyzing. Instead of simply asking yourself "Should I do this?", you answer four specific questions that reveal the full picture.

4 Questions That Will Transform Your Decision

Our brains tend to focus on just one aspect: either the desired outcome or our fears. Descartes' Square forces you to consider every possible scenario.

1

What will happen if I do it?

Positive consequences of action

Describe the benefits of following through with your plan. What will you achieve? What new opportunities will open up? How will you feel?

I'll quit my job and enjoy the freedom of a flexible schedule.
2

What will happen if I DON'T do it?

Positive consequences of inaction

This question is about the upsides of your current situation. What good things will remain in your life if you change nothing? What benefits of stability will you keep?

I'll stay at my job and have a steady paycheck every month.
3

What WON'T happen if I do it?

Negative consequences of action — losses

Be honest with yourself here. What are the risks? What will you lose by taking this step? It could be money, time, comfort, or certain relationships.

I'll lose my guaranteed income and benefits package.
4

What WON'T happen if I DON'T do it?

Negative consequences of inaction — missed opportunities

This is the trickiest question. What do you lose by keeping things as they are? Which dreams will go unfulfilled? What experiences will you miss out on if you don't take the leap?

I'll never find out if I could have built my own business.

How to Use This Tool

1

Define your question

Write down the dilemma that's on your mind. For example: "Should I move to another city?"

2

Fill in all 4 sections

Take your time. Give yourself space to reflect on each square. Try to write specific facts rather than vague generalities.

3

Analyze the results

Look at all four blocks together. The answer often becomes clear once you see the cost of inaction (question 4) or the real risks of action (question 3).

4

Make your decision

Descartes' Square doesn't make the decision for you, but it makes your choice a conscious one.

When Should You Use Descartes' Square?

This method is ideal for major life decisions:

Changing jobs or careers Relocating or buying property Starting or ending a relationship Making significant financial investments Choosing education or training programs
Tip

Don't use this method for minor everyday decisions (like what to have for lunch) — you'll only overload yourself with analysis. Save it for what truly matters.
